**AfterGlow** is a playable simulation about how lighting shapes behavior in public space. The project translates real-world cases of spatial control through illumination into a game system, allowing players to test how light affects movement, rest, visibility, and social response.

Rather than treating light as neutral infrastructure, the project asks: **who controls the light, who is affected by it, and what kinds of behavior does it make possible or impossible?**

## Project Overview

The project began with an observation from a waterfront promenade in Wan Chai, Hong Kong. During the day, small shelters along the water seemed like ideal places to rest: they offered seats, shade, and a view toward the harbor. At night, however, vertical neon panels filled the shelters with intense colored light. The shelters became visually attractive from the outside, but uncomfortable from within. The same lighting that made the space more visible also made resting there difficult.

This contradiction became the core of the project. In **AfterGlow**, lighting is not only visual decoration. It becomes a rule system that changes how a space can be used.

![System diagram showing how real-world lighting conditions are translated into game mechanics.](/public/img/2026/AfterGlow/Diagram.png)

## Design Approach

The project is structured around two playable tracks: an **Engineering Panel** and an **Experience Track**.

In the Engineering Panel, the player acts as an operator. They decide where lights are placed, which lights are turned on, and how brightness and color are adjusted. These choices are not purely aesthetic: they reshape the level by changing visibility, atmosphere, and the social meaning of different zones.

![Engineering Panel interface for controlling light groups, brightness, and color.](/public/img/2026/AfterGlow/Engineer%20Panel.png)

In the Experience Track, the player moves through the station after those lighting decisions have been applied. The same space can feel safe, exposed, hostile, empty, or inviting depending on how it is illuminated. By moving through the level, players experience the effects of lighting decisions from the perspective of a body inside the space.

![Experience Track scene inside the station level.](/public/img/2026/AfterGlow/Level.png)

## Narrative Layer

To connect the system to lived experience, the project includes holographic figures placed near specific lighting conditions. These figures appear like spatial memories. They suggest that every technical decision in the environment also leaves behind a social and emotional trace.

The narrative is intentionally fragmented. Instead of explaining the whole story directly, the project uses light, distance, and encounter to let players discover how control is embedded in the environment.

![Editing a holographic figure in the game engine.](/public/img/2026/AfterGlow/Camera%20Test.png)

## Reflection

**AfterGlow** uses game mechanics to make spatial power testable. By separating the operator’s view from the pedestrian’s experience, the project reveals a gap between designing a controlled environment and living inside it.

The final prototype demonstrates a framework for reading light as both infrastructure and interface: something that organizes space, guides attention, and quietly determines who is allowed to stay.
