# AfterGlow: Lighting as Spatial Control

**AfterGlow** is a playable spatial system that translates real-world cases of control through lighting into a game environment. It asks a simple question: when light shapes visibility, comfort, movement, and rest, who is really controlling the space?

The project began with observations of urban lighting that appears decorative or functional from the outside, but becomes uncomfortable, exposing, or exclusionary for people inside the space. Instead of treating lighting as a neutral environmental layer, **AfterGlow** turns it into a design material, a rule system, and a form of power that players can test directly.

## Concept

Lighting is often discussed through safety, atmosphere, wayfinding, or visual clarity. This project approaches lighting as a spatial mechanism: it can invite people to stay, push them to move, make bodies visible, create zones of comfort, or produce unease. In public space, the same light can be read as decoration, surveillance, care, exclusion, or infrastructure depending on who controls it and who is affected by it.

**AfterGlow** translates this relationship into a game system. Players move through a metro-like environment where light conditions affect navigation, visibility, rest, social response, and spatial access. The goal is not to simulate one real place, but to build a playable framework where lighting becomes legible as a force that organizes behavior.

![A relation diagram analyzing lighting, power, affected bodies, and spatial consequences.](/public/img/2026/AfterGlow/Relation.png)

## Design Research

The research phase focused on the social and emotional effects of urban lighting. Instead of only asking whether a place is bright or dark, the project asks:

- Who owns or controls the light?
- What purpose does the light serve?
- Who is affected by the lighting condition?
- What feeling or behavioral response does the lighting produce?

These questions create a bridge between environmental research and gameplay design. Concepts such as perceived safety, emotional response, visibility, darkness, glare, and atmospheric comfort are converted into interactive rules.

## Spatial Scenario

The current prototype is set in a fictional metro station called **Northline Metro**. The station is clean, quiet, and highly controlled. It contains benches, signs, platform entrances, service passages, illuminated walls, and digital panels. At first glance, it resembles a safe and organized transit space. But when lighting conditions shift, the same station becomes uncomfortable, exposed, or disorienting.

The space is designed around contrasts: bright waiting areas versus dark passages, decorative color lighting versus functional lighting, and visible public zones versus ambiguous transitional zones.

## Level Design

The level is divided into zones that support different forms of interaction and emotional pacing. Some areas encourage waiting, some guide movement, and some produce uncertainty. The map uses station infrastructure as a gameplay layout: entrances, benches, corridors, signage, stairs, and platform access become spatial cues for the player.

![Level zoning map showing the main spatial divisions of the station.](/public/img/2026/AfterGlow/Map1.png)

Lighting zones are mapped separately from architectural zones. This allows the prototype to treat lighting as its own layer of control. The light map defines where illumination changes occur, which areas become comfortable or hostile, and how the player’s route is affected by brightness, color, and visibility.

![Lighting zoning map showing how different lighting areas structure the player experience.](/public/img/2026/AfterGlow/Map2.png)

## Gameplay System

The prototype is organized around three connected layers:

1. **Decision** — the system or player decides which lights turn on, which areas remain dark, and how lighting properties change.
2. **Materialization** — those decisions appear in the environment through brightness, color, contrast, reflection, and visibility.
3. **Evaluation** — the game system calculates how lighting affects movement, comfort, exposure, and spatial behavior.

Through this structure, light is no longer only a visual effect. It becomes a rule-based environmental variable.

![Engineering panel interface used to control lighting conditions in the prototype.](/public/img/2026/AfterGlow/Engineering Panel.png)

The engineering panel allows the player or designer to control lighting groups, adjust light states, and test environmental changes. It works as both a gameplay interface and a design tool, making the power relationship behind lighting more explicit.

## Prototype Experience

The current playable experience focuses on walking through the station, observing lighting changes, and testing how different lighting states alter the atmosphere of the same space. The prototype includes benches, illuminated walls, directional signs, platform entrances, and holographic human figures that represent public presence without becoming fully individualized characters.

![Prototype level scene showing the experiential track and lighting environment.](/public/img/2026/AfterGlow/Level.png)

The holographic figures are used to test how bodies appear under different lighting conditions. Their semi-transparent form emphasizes visibility, exposure, and the relationship between the individual and the illuminated environment.

![Game engine view showing holographic human figures being tested in the scene.](/public/img/2026/AfterGlow/Camera Test.png)

## Visual Direction

The visual direction moves between two conditions. The first is institutional brightness: white surfaces, clear signage, and controlled illumination. The second is atmospheric saturation: colored light, reflective surfaces, darkness, and emotional ambiguity. This contrast allows the project to examine how light can shift a public interior from safe to alienating, or from functional to theatrical.

Rather than presenting lighting as inherently good or bad, **AfterGlow** treats it as relational. A bright space may feel safe to one person and exposing to another. A dark corner may feel dangerous, private, calm, or resistant depending on the player’s goal and the surrounding system.

## Future Development

The project can be expanded into a broader game structure with more interactive systems, narrative events, environmental responses, and social consequences. Future development may include non-player character behavior, route choice, adaptive lighting, resource management, and stronger connections between lighting decisions and player outcomes.

![Future development diagram showing possible expansions from the current game architecture.](/public/img/2026/AfterGlow/Next Step.png)

Possible next steps include:

- Expanding the station into a larger transit network.
- Adding NPC reactions to lighting changes.
- Connecting lighting decisions to narrative consequences.
- Developing a clearer resource or economy system around energy, control, and access.
- Testing how different player roles experience the same lighting system differently.

## Conclusion

**AfterGlow** is a design experiment, a game prototype, and a spatial argument. It uses playable interaction to show that lighting is not only a technical or aesthetic layer of public space. It is also a social interface that can guide, expose, comfort, exclude, and control.

By turning lighting into a game system, the project allows players to experience spatial control not as an abstract concept, but as something they can move through, modify, and question.
